## A comprehensive list of financial assets:

### 1. Cash and Cash Equivalents

These are the most liquid and generally considered the safest investments, offering low returns. They are primarily used for capital preservation and short-term needs.

- **Savings Accounts:** A deposit account held at a bank or other financial institution.
- **Money Market Funds / Liquid Funds:** A type of mutual fund that invests in high-quality, short-term debt instruments.
- **Certificates of Deposit (CDs) / Fixed Deposits (FDs):** A time deposit with a financial institution that pays a fixed interest rate for a specified period.
- **Treasury Bills (T-Bills):** Short-term debt securities issued by a national government with maturities of one year or less.
- **Commercial Paper:** Short-term, unsecured promissory notes issued by corporations to finance short-term liabilities like payroll.

### 2. Fixed-Income Instruments (Debt/Bonds)

When you buy a fixed-income security, you are essentially lending money to an entity (government or corporation) in exchange for periodic interest payments and the return of the principal at maturity.

- **Government Bonds:**

  - **Treasury Notes (T-Notes) & Bonds (T-Bonds):** Mid- to long-term debt issued by a national government (e.g., US Treasuries, Indian G-Secs). Considered very safe.
  - **State and Municipal Bonds ("Munis"):** Issued by state or local governments to fund public projects. In some countries, the interest income is tax-exempt.
  - **Inflation-Protected Bonds (e.g., TIPS in the US):** The principal value adjusts with inflation, protecting the investor from a decline in purchasing power.
  - **Sovereign Bonds:** Bonds issued by foreign governments.

- **Corporate Bonds:**

  - **Investment-Grade Bonds:** Issued by financially stable companies with a high credit rating.
  - **High-Yield Bonds ("Junk Bonds"):** Issued by companies with a lower credit rating, offering higher interest rates to compensate for higher risk.
  - **Convertible Bonds:** Can be converted into a predetermined number of common stock shares of the issuing company.
  - **Callable Bonds:** Can be redeemed or "called" by the issuer before the bond's maturity date.

- **Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS):** A pool of mortgages bundled together and sold as a single investment.
- **Asset-Backed Securities (ABS):** Similar to MBS but backed by other assets like auto loans, credit card receivables, or student loans.
- **Peer-to-Peer (P2P) Lending:** Directly lending money to individuals or small businesses through online platforms, bypassing traditional banks.

### 3. Equities (Stocks)

Equities represent an ownership stake in a publicly traded company. They offer the potential for high growth but come with higher risk.

- **Common Stock:** Represents ownership, provides voting rights, and entitles the holder to a share of the company's profits through dividends.
- **Preferred Stock:** A hybrid security with features of both debt and equity. It pays a fixed dividend and has a higher claim on assets than common stock but usually has no voting rights.
- **Warrants:** A security that entitles the holder to buy the underlying stock of the issuing company at a fixed price until an expiry date.
- **Stock Rights:** A right issued by a company to existing shareholders to buy more shares at a discount to the market price.

Equities can also be classified by:

- **Market Capitalization:** Large-Cap, Mid-Cap, Small-Cap.
- **Geography:** Domestic, International (Developed Markets, Emerging Markets).
- **Investment Style:** Growth Stocks, Value Stocks, Dividend (Income) Stocks.

### 4. Pooled Investment Vehicles (Funds)

These are professionally managed portfolios that pool money from many investors to purchase a diversified collection of securities.

- **Mutual Funds:**
  - **Equity Funds, Debt Funds, Hybrid Funds.**
  - **Actively Managed vs. Index Funds (Passively Managed).**
  - **Open-End Funds vs. Closed-End Funds.**
- **Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs):** A basket of securities that trades on an exchange like a stock. They can track an index, sector, commodity, or other assets.
- **Hedge Funds:** Actively managed investment funds that use advanced and often aggressive strategies. Typically open only to accredited or high-net-worth investors.
- **Private Equity Funds:** Funds that invest directly in private companies or engage in buyouts of public companies.
- **Venture Capital (VC) Funds:** A subset of private equity that provides capital to startups and small businesses with high growth potential.
- **Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs):** Companies that own or finance income-producing real estate across a range of property sectors. They trade on major exchanges like stocks.

### 5. Derivatives

These are complex financial contracts whose value is derived from an underlying asset, such as a stock, bond, or commodity. They are primarily used for hedging risk or for speculation.

- **Options:** Gives the buyer the right, but not the obligation, to buy (a 'call' option) or sell (a 'put' option) an asset at a specified price on or before a certain date.
- **Futures:** A legal agreement to buy or sell a particular commodity or financial instrument at a predetermined price at a specified time in the future.
- **Forwards:** A customized, private contract between two parties to buy or sell an asset at a specified price on a future date. Unlike futures, they are not traded on a central exchange.
- **Swaps:** A contract through which two parties exchange financial instruments or cashflows for a certain time. Common types include interest rate swaps and currency swaps.

### 6. Real Assets and Commodities

These are investments in tangible, physical assets.

- **Real Estate:**
  - **Direct Ownership:** Residential properties, commercial buildings, or raw land.
  - **Real Estate Crowdfunding:** Pooling capital with other investors online to invest in property projects.
- **Commodities:** Raw materials or primary agricultural products.
  - **Precious Metals:** Gold, Silver, Platinum, Palladium.
  - **Energy:** Crude Oil, Natural Gas.
  - **Industrial Metals:** Copper, Aluminum, Zinc.
  - **Agriculture:** Corn, Wheat, Soybeans, Coffee, Sugar.
  - _Note: Direct investment often happens via futures contracts, ETFs, or stocks of companies that produce these commodities._
- **Collectibles:** Tangible items that are collected and have potential investment value. This is a highly specialized and illiquid market.
  - Fine Art, Antiques, Classic Cars, Rare Wines, Stamps, Coins.
- **Timberland and Farmland:** Direct investment in land used for growing timber or crops.

### 7. Digital Assets

A new and evolving asset class based on blockchain and cryptographic technology. These are highly volatile and speculative.

- **Cryptocurrencies:** Digital or virtual tokens that use cryptography for security (e.g., Bitcoin - $BTC$, Ethereum - $ETH$).
- **Stablecoins:** Cryptocurrencies designed to minimize volatility by being pegged to a stable asset, like a fiat currency (e.g., Tether - $USDT$, USD Coin - $USDC$).
- **Non-Fungible Tokens (NFTs):** Unique cryptographic tokens that represent ownership of a specific digital or physical item.
- **Decentralized Finance (DeFi) Products:** Financial services built on blockchain technology, such as staking, yield farming, and liquidity pools, which allow asset holders to earn returns.

### 8. Alternative and Other Miscellaneous Assets

A broad category for sophisticated investments that do not fit into the other categories.

- **Structured Products:** Pre-packaged investments created by financial institutions that combine elements of traditional assets (like bonds) with derivatives. An example is a Principal-Protected Note (PPN).
- **Intellectual Property (IP):** Investing in the rights to receive royalties from patents, trademarks, copyrights (e.g., music royalties).
- **Private Debt/Credit:** Directly lending to private companies, which is not available on public markets.
- **Annuities:** A contract with an insurance company where you make a lump-sum payment or series of payments and, in return, receive regular disbursements, beginning either immediately or at some point in the future.

**Generally Restricted for Trading by Indian Residents:**

According to Indian regulations, resident individuals are generally **prohibited** from trading in the following products on overseas markets:

- **Futures:** Trading in international futures contracts is not permitted.
- **Options:** Trading in international options contracts is not permitted.
- **Margin-based Products and CFDs:** Any form of speculative trading on margin, including Contracts for Differences (CFDs) on forex, indices, or stocks, is not allowed.
- **Forex Trading (Speculative):** While you can convert currency for the purpose of investing in permissible assets, speculative forex trading (trading currency pairs) is not permitted for resident Indians.

### **Important Considerations for Indian Investors**

- **Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS):** All your overseas investments are subject to the LRS limit, which is currently USD 250,000 per financial year.
- **Taxation:** You are liable to pay taxes in India on your global income, which includes capital gains and dividends from your international investments. It's advisable to consult with a tax advisor to understand your tax obligations.
- **Account Funding:** You will need to remit funds from your Indian bank account to your Interactive Brokers global account in compliance with FEMA (Foreign Exchange Management Act) regulations.
